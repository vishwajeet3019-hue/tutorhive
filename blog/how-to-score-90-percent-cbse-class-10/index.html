How to Score 90% in CBSE Class 10 Boards: Study Plan and Subject Tips

Scoring 90% or above in CBSE Class 10 boards is achievable with a structured plan, strong concept clarity, and consistent practice — not last-minute cramming. This guide covers subject-by-subject strategies, a realistic preparation timeline, and the revision approach that separates high scorers from average ones.

What 90% actually requires — and what most students get wrong

Most students preparing for CBSE Class 10 boards focus too heavily on coverage — finishing every chapter — and too little on depth. A student who has read all 15 chapters of Social Studies but cannot write a structured 5-mark answer reliably will score 65–70%, not 90%.

What separates 90%+ scorers is answer quality, not knowledge quantity. CBSE board examiners follow a marking scheme strictly. Knowing the right information but presenting it poorly — missing keywords, giving incomplete steps in maths, writing vague answers in science — costs marks that cannot be recovered.

The second thing 90% requires is zero chapter blind spots in maths and science. A student who skips two chapters in Class 10 maths hoping they do not appear will likely lose 10–15 marks, which alone pulls the aggregate below 90%. Breadth of preparation matters in these two subjects.

Realistic preparation timeline for CBSE Class 10 boards

Start of Class 10 (June–August): Build foundations chapter by chapter. Do not rush. Each chapter understood deeply in this phase saves twice the revision time later. Pay particular attention to maths chapters — Real Numbers, Polynomials, Pair of Linear Equations — which form the base for everything that follows.

Term 1 exams (October–November): Treat these as a full dress rehearsal, not just school tests. Review your answer sheets critically. Marks lost in Term 1 exams are almost always lost in the same way in boards — incomplete steps, missed diagrams, unclear explanations.

November–January: Complete the full syllabus, including chapters that feel less important. In Science, do not skip Biology chapters like Life Processes or Reproduction. In Social Studies, do not skip Economics. These chapters appear every year and carry significant weightage.

January–February: Shift entirely to previous years' papers and revision. Stop reading new material. Every hour in this phase should be either solving a paper under timed conditions or reviewing answers against the marking scheme.

Two weeks before boards: One full revision of formulae, definitions, diagrams, and answer outlines. Sleep and consistency matter more than intensity in this phase.

Maths: how to score 90+ out of 80

CBSE Class 10 maths is marked out of 80 (with 20 for internal assessment). To score 90% overall, you need roughly 72–74 out of 80 in the board exam. This requires near-perfect accuracy in the 1-mark and 2-mark sections and solid performance across all 5-mark questions.

Do not skip steps. CBSE maths marking schemes award marks for intermediate steps, not just the final answer. A student who writes only the answer for a 3-mark problem and gets it right still scores 1–2 marks. Show every step, clearly.

Master the high-weightage chapters first. Triangles, Circles, Arithmetic Progressions, Quadratic Equations, Coordinate Geometry, and Statistics collectively carry more than 50% of the marks. These chapters must be thorough.

Solve CBSE previous years' papers, not just NCERT. NCERT exercises are necessary but not sufficient. Board questions are phrased differently, often combining two concepts. Practice on actual board papers from the last 5 years builds the pattern recognition needed for speed and accuracy.

Time management in the exam. Class 10 maths is a 3-hour paper. Allocate 10 minutes for reading, 30 minutes for Section A (MCQs and short answers), 60 minutes for Section B, and 70 minutes for Section C. Keep 10 minutes at the end for review. Students who run out of time on the 5-mark questions lose the most marks.

Science: how to approach all three sections

CBSE Class 10 Science covers Physics, Chemistry, and Biology across 13 chapters. The marking distribution is roughly equal across the three sections, so ignoring any one of them is expensive.

Physics (Light, Electricity, Magnetic Effects): These chapters are calculation-heavy and diagram-heavy. Practise ray diagrams for mirrors and lenses until you can draw them correctly without referring to notes. For electricity and magnetism, understand the concepts behind each formula — do not memorise formulas in isolation.

Chemistry (Chemical Reactions, Acids and Bases, Metals and Non-Metals, Carbon Compounds): This section rewards students who learn equation balancing and reaction types thoroughly. Practise writing and balancing chemical equations daily in the two months before boards. Learn the properties and reactions of metals vs non-metals as a comparative table — it makes answers faster to recall.

Biology (Life Processes, Control and Coordination, Reproduction, Heredity): Diagrams are the fastest marks in biology. The heart, nephron, neuron, flower diagram, and DNA replication diagram appear consistently. A well-labelled, neat diagram with a one-line explanation earns full marks and takes less time to write than a paragraph answer.

CBSE Science answer format: Board examiners follow strict marking schemes. Each point in a 3-mark or 5-mark answer must be distinct. Use bullet points or numbered steps for process-based answers. Write definitions exactly — vague paraphrasing loses marks on definition questions.

English: how to score consistently high

English is the subject where a methodical student can score 90–95 most reliably. The Class 10 English paper has a clear, predictable structure: Reading Comprehension, Writing (letters, articles, paragraphs), Grammar, and Literature.

Reading Comprehension: The answers are in the passage — you do not need prior knowledge. The skill is finding the right line and rephrasing it clearly. Practise answering reading comprehension questions by underlining the relevant sentence in the passage first, then writing the answer in your own words. Quoting the passage verbatim often loses marks.

Writing section: Letters, formal and informal, follow a fixed format. Formal letters need address, date, subject line, salutation, body, closing. Learn the format exactly and use it every time. Articles and paragraphs follow an introduction-body-conclusion structure. Strong students lose marks here by not following format strictly.

Grammar: Subject-verb agreement, tenses, reported speech, and sentence transformation are the most commonly tested areas. These are learnable rules — 20–30 minutes of daily grammar practice in the final two months before boards will make this section reliable.

Literature: Prepare answers for every poem and prose chapter in First Flight and Footprints Without Feet. For short answer questions, always refer to the specific event, character, or line the question is asking about. Generic answers about theme or character without textual reference lose marks.

Social Studies: the subject most students underestimate

Social Studies (History, Political Science, Geography, Economics) is often treated as the easiest subject, but it is also where many students fail to reach their potential because of poor answer structure rather than lack of knowledge.

Answer writing is the core skill. A 3-mark SST answer needs three distinct points. A 5-mark answer needs five, ideally with sub-headings or clear numbering. Students who write long, flowing paragraphs without clear points often score 2 on a 3-mark question despite knowing the answer.

Maps are easy full marks. Geography map practice — rivers, mountains, states, crops, minerals — takes 10–15 minutes per day and returns 5 marks on the board exam. Almost no student skips maps and still scores 90% overall.

Do not ignore Economics. Development, Sectors of the Indian Economy, Money and Credit, and Globalisation appear every year with significant weightage. The chapters are not long but the terminology — GDP, formal sector, MNC — must be defined correctly.

Revision strategy: how to revise so it sticks

Most students revise by re-reading. Re-reading feels productive but produces poor retention. Instead, use active recall: close the textbook, write down everything you remember about a chapter, then check what you missed. This identifies gaps far more reliably than re-reading.

Chapter summaries in your own words. After completing each chapter, write a one-page summary in your own language — not copied from the textbook. This forces you to process the information rather than passively receive it.

Formula and definition sheets. Maintain a running sheet of maths formulae, science definitions, and SST key terms. Revise this sheet daily in the final month. Spaced repetition — reviewing the same information across multiple days — builds durable memory.

Previous years' papers under timed conditions. Solve papers under strict exam conditions: no phone, no breaks, full 3 hours. After finishing, mark your own paper against the CBSE marking scheme available on the CBSE website. The self-marking step is where most of the learning happens.

The role of doubt clearing in reaching 90%

The difference between a student who scores 78% and one who scores 91% is almost always concept gaps that were never identified and fixed. Every wrong answer in a maths paper or incomplete answer in science traces back to a specific concept that was not properly understood.

A student who consistently gets geometry questions wrong is not "bad at geometry" — they are missing a specific logical step, usually in proof writing or angle relationships. Finding and fixing that step with a tutor takes one or two sessions. Not finding it costs 10–15 marks across the paper.

Frequently asked questions

Is it possible to score 90% in CBSE Class 10 boards without tuition?

Yes, it is possible — but it requires strong self-discipline, consistent revision, and regular practice with previous years' papers. Many students who score 90%+ do have support in at least one or two subjects where they have gaps, because concept clarity in maths and science is hard to build alone without feedback.

Which subjects matter most for scoring 90% in CBSE Class 10?

Maths and Science are the two subjects that most often pull the aggregate down, because scoring low in either has a big impact on the overall percentage. English and Social Studies are typically easier to score high in with consistent practice of writing and answer structuring.

How many months before board exams should a Class 10 student start serious preparation?

Ideally, serious preparation begins at the start of Class 10 itself — building strong foundations in Term 1 means Term 2 can focus on integration and practice. If starting late, 4–5 months before the board exam is the minimum to cover the full syllabus, solve previous years' papers, and revise twice.

How many previous years' papers should a student solve for CBSE Class 10?

Solving 5–7 years of previous CBSE board papers per subject is a strong target. This covers enough variety in question patterns to reduce surprises in the actual exam. Always solve papers under timed, exam conditions rather than open-book.
