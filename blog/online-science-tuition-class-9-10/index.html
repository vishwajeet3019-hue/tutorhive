Online Science Tuition for Class 9 and 10: Physics, Chemistry, Biology Guide

CBSE Class 9 and 10 science splits into three distinct sections — Physics, Chemistry, and Biology — each with its own style of thinking, problem types, and exam format. Most students are strong in one section and struggling in another. This guide explains what makes each section hard, which chapters cause the most trouble, and how 1:1 online science tuition addresses gaps that classroom teaching misses.

Why science becomes harder from Class 9 onwards

Up to Class 8, science is a single subject — a blend of facts, experiments, and simple explanations. From Class 9, CBSE science divides into Physics, Chemistry, and Biology as three separate intellectual disciplines. Physics becomes calculation-based, requiring algebra and formula application. Chemistry introduces chemical equations, reactions, and element properties that must be memorised and applied. Biology shifts from general observations to detailed cellular and organ-system understanding.

This split is the source of most Class 9 science struggles. A student who enjoyed the descriptive parts of Class 8 science finds Class 9 Physics unexpectedly hard. A student comfortable with Biology is frustrated by Chemistry equation balancing. The common pattern: one section pulls the aggregate down while the other two are manageable.

In Class 10, the same three sections continue but with higher complexity. Physics adds Electricity and Magnetic Effects. Chemistry adds Carbon Compounds. Biology adds Heredity and Evolution. Students who did not consolidate Class 9 foundations find Class 10 compounding those gaps rapidly.

Physics: what makes it hard and how tuition helps

Class 9 Physics chapters: Motion, Force and Laws of Motion, Gravitation, Work Energy and Power, Sound.

Class 10 Physics chapters: Light (Reflection and Refraction), Human Eye, Electricity, Magnetic Effects of Electric Current, Sources of Energy.

Physics is hard because it requires two simultaneous skills: conceptual understanding of what is happening (why does a lens form an image here?) and mathematical accuracy in calculation (getting the sign convention right in the mirror formula). Students who understand the concept but make calculation errors, and students who can substitute into formulas but do not understand what they are calculating, both lose marks — in different question types.

Motion (Class 9): Distance-time and velocity-time graphs, equations of motion, and numerical problems. The graphs are the most commonly missed area — students can memorise the equations but cannot interpret a curved distance-time graph or calculate displacement from a velocity-time graph's area.

Electricity (Class 10): Ohm's Law, resistance in series and parallel, power calculations, and heating effect. Series vs parallel resistance — knowing when to add directly and when to use the reciprocal — is the most common source of errors. Students who learn the formulas without understanding the circuit behaviour make the same error repeatedly.

Light (Class 10): Ray diagrams for mirrors and lenses, sign convention, and the mirror/lens formula. Diagram accuracy is everything. A student who cannot draw the three principal rays for a convex lens cannot visualise the image position, so any numerical substitution is a guess.

A tutor watching a student solve Physics numericals live can catch formula confusion, sign errors, and diagram mistakes immediately — before they become habits that cost marks across multiple questions in the exam.

Chemistry: the section most students underestimate

Class 9 Chemistry chapters: Matter in Our Surroundings, Is Matter Around Us Pure, Atoms and Molecules, Structure of the Atom.

Class 10 Chemistry chapters: Chemical Reactions and Equations, Acids Bases and Salts, Metals and Non-Metals, Carbon and its Compounds, Classification of Elements.

Chemistry in Class 9 is largely conceptual — states of matter, mixtures and solutions, atomic structure. Students often find this manageable. The step change comes in Class 10, where chemical equations become central. Balancing equations, identifying reaction types (combination, decomposition, displacement), and writing word equations for described reactions require systematic practice that many students never develop.

Balancing chemical equations: This is a daily practice skill, not a concept to understand once. Students who balance 5 equations understand the idea but make errors under pressure. Students who balance 50 equations across a month do it automatically. Most classroom teaching covers the concept but does not build the practice volume.

Acids, Bases, and Salts: The pH scale, indicators, reaction types, and salt formation. The common error is confusing acid-base neutralisation with other displacement reactions. Students also frequently mix up the properties of strong and weak acids, or acidic and alkaline solutions in the context of everyday examples.

Metals and Non-Metals: Reactivity series, reactions with oxygen, water, and acids, and properties of ionic compounds. This chapter is heavily fact-based but the exam tests application — predicting whether a reaction will occur, writing the product formula. Students who memorise the reactivity series without understanding why it orders elements that way cannot answer application questions.

Carbon Compounds: Homologous series, nomenclature, functional groups, and properties of ethanol and ethanoic acid. The naming rules — substituting -ane, -ene, -ol, -al, -oic acid — are applied to unfamiliar compounds in board questions. Students who understand the system can handle new compounds; students who memorised specific names cannot.

Biology: where marks are easiest to gain and easiest to lose

Class 9 Biology chapters: Cell — The Fundamental Unit of Life, Tissues, Diversity in Living Organisms, Why Do We Fall Ill, Natural Resources, Improvement in Food Resources.

Class 10 Biology chapters: Life Processes, Control and Coordination, Reproduction, Heredity and Evolution, Our Environment, Sustainable Management.

Biology is the section where a methodical student can score the most reliably. The content is largely factual and conceptual — no calculations beyond basic genetics ratios. But it is also the section where students who rely on vague memory lose the most marks, because CBSE biology questions test specific terminology, labelled diagrams, and step-by-step process descriptions.

Diagrams: The human heart, nephron, neuron, flower, germinating seed, and reflex arc appear consistently across board years. A clean, well-labelled diagram with arrows in the correct direction earns full diagram marks quickly. Students who sketch rough diagrams or skip labels lose 1–2 marks on every diagram question, which adds up across a paper.

Life Processes (Class 10): Nutrition, respiration, transport, and excretion each require process descriptions with correct sequencing. Photosynthesis and the double circulation of blood are the two most tested processes. Students who understand the mechanism can answer any variation of these questions; students who memorise a fixed answer are confused when the question changes format.

Heredity (Class 10): Monohybrid and dihybrid crosses, dominant and recessive traits, and the reasoning behind ratios. The Punnett square is the most tested tool. Students must be able to construct a cross, fill the square correctly, and state the genotypic and phenotypic ratios — all for a typical 5-mark question.

How 1:1 online science tuition works differently from classroom teaching

The fundamental advantage of 1:1 science tuition is that the tutor knows exactly which section — Physics, Chemistry, or Biology — is behind, and within that section, which chapters. A classroom teacher teaching 40 students cannot differentiate: the lesson moves at the class's average pace.

In practice, a 1:1 online science session looks like this: the tutor gives the student an unseen question from the chapter being studied. The student attempts it while sharing their screen or writing on a shared whiteboard. The tutor watches exactly where the student stops — which formula they reach for, which diagram they draw, which step they skip. That specific moment is the teaching point.

This feedback loop — attempt, observe error, address that specific error, retry — is impossible in a class setting and is what produces the fastest improvement in students who have real concept gaps rather than just revision needs.

Should science tuition start in Class 9 or wait until Class 10?

Starting in Class 9 is strongly recommended. The three sections — Physics, Chemistry, Biology — appear as CBSE board subjects in Class 10. Gaps formed in Class 9 carry directly into board preparation. A student who does not understand Motion in Class 9 will find Electricity and Light in Class 10 harder, because the problem-solving approach is the same. A student who cannot balance equations in Class 9 will struggle with the entire Chemistry section of Class 10 boards.

Students who start science tuition in Class 9 enter Class 10 with clean chapter coverage. They can spend the board year on practice and paper work rather than rebuilding foundations. Students who wait until Class 10 to address Class 9 gaps are doing both simultaneously — an avoidable pressure.

Frequently asked questions

Which part of science is hardest in Class 9 and 10 — Physics, Chemistry, or Biology?

It varies by student, but Physics is most commonly cited as the hardest because it combines conceptual understanding with calculation. Many students find Chemistry's equation balancing frustrating until they practise consistently. Biology is generally the most manageable but requires precise diagram drawing and definition recall under exam conditions.

Can one online tutor cover all three sections of science?

Some tutors are strong across all three sections of Class 9 and 10 science, but it is worth asking specifically about their Physics and Chemistry strength, as these are the most commonly under-taught. At TutorHive, we match students with tutors based on specific subject gaps rather than assigning a generalist.

How many science tuition sessions per week does a Class 9 or 10 student need?

For a student with moderate gaps, 2–3 sessions per week is effective — one session each for Physics and Chemistry, with Biology integrated or alternated. Students with large gaps or approaching board exams may need 4 sessions per week to cover revision and previous years' paper practice alongside new chapters.

Is online science tuition effective compared to in-person coaching?

Yes — for 1:1 tuition, online delivery is as effective as in-person. Diagrams, equations, and problems can all be shared and worked through on screen. The key advantage of online 1:1 over in-person coaching centres is individual attention: the tutor addresses your child's specific gaps rather than teaching to the whole group.

Should science tuition start in Class 9 or wait until Class 10?

Starting in Class 9 is strongly recommended. Gaps formed in Class 9 Physics, Chemistry, and Biology carry directly into Class 10 board preparation. Fixing them in Class 9 is far less stressful than fixing them during the board year.
