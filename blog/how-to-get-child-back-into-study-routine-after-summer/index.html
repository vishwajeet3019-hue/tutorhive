How to Get Your Child Back Into a Study Routine After Summer Holidays

After two months of unstructured time, children rarely ease back into studying on their own. The good news is that a calm, gradual approach works far better than a sudden strict timetable.

Schools in India reopen every June, and most parents face the same challenge: a child who stayed up late, watched screens freely, and had no study pressure for weeks is now expected to sit down and focus on Chapter 1 immediately. That gap between holiday mode and school mode does not close overnight, but it does close — with the right steps.

1. Fix the sleep and wake cycle before anything else

Study routine begins with a sleep routine. A child who has been sleeping at midnight and waking at 9 a.m. cannot suddenly switch to a 6 a.m. wake-up and concentrate in school by 8 a.m.

Start shifting bedtime and wake time by 30 minutes every two to three days in the week before school reopens. This is quiet, low-conflict work that makes everything else — focus, memory, mood — noticeably easier once classes begin.

2. Begin with a single study block, not a full timetable

One common mistake is printing a detailed timetable and expecting it to hold. Children who have had total freedom resist structure when it is introduced all at once. Begin with one block of 30 to 45 minutes at a fixed time each day — ideally after a meal and a short break, not immediately after school.

Let the block be consistent for the first week. Add a second block in the second week. By the time unit tests arrive in July or August, the habit already exists instead of being built under pressure. 3. Start with subjects your child finds easier

Opening a maths textbook on day one after two months off is a fast way to create resistance. Start with a subject your child is more comfortable with — English reading, social studies, or light revision of last year's work. Building confidence in the first week makes it significantly easier to tackle harder subjects in the second.

This is not about avoiding difficult work. It is about removing the immediate association between studying and feeling stuck, which is the quickest way to shut motivation down.

4. Reintroduce reading before textbooks

Reading — even non-academic books, newspapers, or age-appropriate magazines — reactivates concentration and vocabulary that has gone quiet during the holidays. Children who read regularly for 15 to 20 minutes per day maintain significantly stronger comprehension and answer-writing ability than those who do not.

If your child resists reading altogether, try ten minutes of reading aloud together. The shared activity reduces the feeling of being made to do something and often leads to independent reading more quickly.

5. Set screen time rules before school starts

Removing screens completely causes conflict. Replacing them with a predictable structure does not. The simplest approach: screens begin only after the day's study block is complete. Children who know exactly when their screen time starts are far less likely to negotiate during study time.

For children in Classes 8, 9, and 10, where the temptation to use phones during study is stronger, a separate device-free study zone at a table or desk works better than a rule applied to their room.

6. Prepare the study space before week one

A cleared table, good lighting, and the right books within reach removes the small friction that gives children an easy reason to delay starting. It takes ten minutes to set up but removes a daily source of procrastination.

If your child is doing online tuition, check that the device, headphones, and internet connection are working reliably before the first session. A technical problem in the first class is a significant mood disruption at the start of a new term.

7. Do a light recall session before teaching new chapters

Before jumping into new syllabus chapters, spend one session asking your child what they remember from last year. Not testing — just asking them to talk through it. This does two things: it shows you what has been retained and what needs rebuilding, and it gives the child a confidence boost from remembering more than they expected.

If several concepts have genuinely been forgotten — especially in maths or science — targeted revision of those specific gaps is more efficient than re-doing entire chapters. A good tutor can identify those gaps in the first session and build a plan around only what is missing.

8. Keep the first two weeks low-pressure

Resist the urge to load every available hour with study in the first fortnight. Children need some adjustment time. Overloading the transition period can create negative associations with studying that take much longer to undo than the two weeks saved would be worth.

The goal in weeks one and two is a functioning routine, not full academic intensity. Full intensity can come in week three, once the habit is established.

When your child needs more support than routine alone

If the summer break has left clear gaps — a child in Class 7 who has genuinely forgotten fractions, or a Class 9 student who has no revision of Class 8 science before the new chapters begin — routine alone will not close those gaps before unit tests arrive.

Frequently asked questions

How long does it take for a child to get back into a study routine after summer?

Most children settle within one to two weeks if the transition is gradual. Starting before school reopens — even with 20 minutes of daily reading — removes the cold-start shock on day one.

Should I start a strict timetable right after summer?

A full timetable introduced suddenly often fails. Build one block at a fixed time in week one, add a second in week two, and let the structure grow before tests begin.

My child has forgotten a lot from last year. What should I do?

Some forgetting is normal. Start with light recall conversations rather than re-teaching everything. A tutor can identify specific gaps and rebuild only what is genuinely missing rather than covering entire chapters again.

How do I reduce screen time without constant arguments?

Give screens a predictable window after the study block is done — not before. Children negotiate less when they know exactly when screen time begins, rather than when it depends on a judgment call.
